Understanding Piezo 1 and Piezo 2 Ion Channels

The Science Behind Vibrotactile Technology

First discovered by Ardem Patapoutian and his team in 2010 (resulting in the receipt of a Nobel Prize in 2021), Piezo-1 and 2 were identified as ion channels in the plasma membrane of neuronal cells that can be activated in response to stimuli such as temperature, chemicals, and mechanical force.1 Moreover, Piezo-1 and 2 together were discovered to be the most major non-selective mechano-sensitive ion cation channels in the cell membrane.2 (Cation channels are protein pores embedded in a neuron's membrane that allows passage into and through it of positively-charged ions.) In contrast to Piezo-1 (which senses mechanical forces such as pressure in non-sensory cells [e.g., lung, bladder, and skin]), Piezo-2 is primarily found in sensory neurons. Importantly for its impact on recent haptic technology developments, Piezo-2 is especially found in dorsal root ganglion (DRG) neurons and also Merkel cells (found right below the skin's epidermis, and interacting with nerve endings).2

Outcomes from multiple studies evaluating Vibrotactile Haptic Technology have shown positive results with those subjects using patches for pain, sleep, balance, and stress/anxiety.3-7,10 Additional randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are currently taking place.
